Afghanistan 33
Da Afġānistān Islāmī Imārat · South-Central Asia · Capital: Kabul
Central Asia

At a Glance

Capital: Kabul — lies in a valley of the Hindu Kush. (4)

Government: Controlled by the Taliban; flag changed to Arabic letters on white. President Ashraf Ghani fled in 2021. (3)

Languages: Official languages Dari and Pashto. (1)

Borders: Pakistan to S/E (along the Durand Line); the Wakhan Corridor narrow strip connects Afghanistan to China. (8)

Physical Geography

Hindu Kush — dominant mountain range; western end inside Afghanistan; contains the Wakhan Corridor, Badakhshan region, and peaks Tirich Mir and Noshaq; the Khyber Pass crosses it. (5)

Wakhan Corridor — 11-mi-wide salient in Badakhshan Province created as a 'Great Game' buffer between Russian and British empires (1895); contains Lake Zorkul, Noshaq peak, and the Wakhjir Pass (largest time-zone jump in the world); bounded by the Pamir and Panj Rivers. (5)

Khyber Pass — strategic mountain pass on the Pakistan border; through the Safed Koh (Spin Gar) range; eastern end at Peshawar; western end at Jalalabad; lies above Jamrud Fortress; famed by Kipling as a 'sword cut'; site of the Torkham Highway. (4)

Hari River (Rudkaneh-ye Hari Rud) — Herat lies on it; flows NW into Turkmenistan, watering the breadbasket region. (1)

Cities & Regions

Kabul — capital; in a Hindu-Kush valley; Hamid Karzai International Airport site of an Islamic-State-Khorasan attack. (2)

Kandahar — cultural center of the Pashtun ethnic group; second-largest city. (2)

Herat — third-largest city, in the far NW; Tajik-majority; on the Hari River; once a Timurid capital; Musalla Complex razed in the 1885 'Panjdeh incident' (Great Game). (3)

Mazar-e-Sharif — in Balkh province; Shrine of Hazrat Ali with twin blue domes. (2)

Kunduz — northern city. (1)

Bagram Airfield — large U.S. military base in Parwan province (now Taliban-held). (1)

Bamiyan Valley — once held giant standing Buddha statues; destroyed by the Taliban in 2001; cultural center of the Hazara ethnic group. (2)

Kabul–Kandahar highway — major resurfacing project (Louis Berger Group); site of attacks. (1)

People

Pashtuns — majority ethnic group; folk dance is the attan; Sharbat Gula ('Afghan Girl' on the iconic National Geographic cover) is Pashtun. (1)

Hazaras — Shia Muslim minority; concentrated in Daikundi, Bamyan, and Ghor provinces; some Behsud tribe. (1)

Tajiks — dominate Balkh and Herat provinces. (2)

Algeria 25

Middle East & North Africa

At a Glance

Capital: Algiers — on the Mediterranean coast. (5)

Distinction: Largest country in Africa by area; the largest of the historical Barbary Coast. (1)

Position: Intersection of the Tropic of Cancer and the Prime Meridian lies in this country. (1)

Borders: Tunisia (E), Libya (E), Niger/Mali (S), Mauritania/Western Sahara/Morocco (W). (2)

Physical Geography

Atlas Mountains — span Algeria with Morocco and Tunisia; the Aurès Mountains are the eastern continuation; the Tell Atlas runs near the coast; the god Atlas of Greek myth gives the range its name. (4)

Ahaggar Mountains — in the south; contain Mt. Tahat, the country's highest point. (1)

Sahara Desert — covers most of Algeria; includes the two great sand-seas (ergs): Grand Erg Occidental and Grand Erg Oriental; the Tademait Plateau has star and barchan dunes. (2)

M'zab Valley — five fortified villages collectively called the Pentapolis; Ghardaïa is a notable M'zab city. (2)

Algiers

Set on a hill above the Mediterranean; iconic Casbah old town; Cathedral of Notre Dame d'Afrique (Neo-Byzantine, a relic of French colonization). (3)

Battle of Algiers (1956–57) — French counter-insurgency in the war for independence; subject of Gillo Pontecorvo's 1966 film The Battle of Algiers (Golden Lion). (3)

Algeria's pied-noir French-descended population fled after independence in 1962. (1)

Once ruled by a Dey; Miguel de Cervantes was held captive here. (1)

World's tallest minaret towers above Algiers (Djamaâ el Djazaïr mosque). (1)

Other Cities

Oran — major port on the Mediterranean; setting of Albert Camus' The Plague; site where Churchill destroyed the French fleet at Mers-el-Kébir in 1940; oil/gas industry centered here (state company Sonatrach in the suburb of Bir El-Djir). (3)

Constantine and Annaba — major eastern cities. (2)

Évian Accords (1962) — signed in France, recognized Algerian independence. (2)

Andaman & Nicobar Islands 18
Union Territory in the Bay of Bengal · Capital: Port Blair
India (States & Regions)

At a Glance

Capital: Port Blair — on the Andaman Islands. (6)

Status: Union Territory of India, in the eastern Bay of Bengal / Andaman Sea. (5)

Two island groups: Andaman Islands (N), Nicobar Islands (S), separated by the Ten Degree Channel. (4)

Highest point: Saddle Peak (Andamans); also home of the creator god Puluga worshipped by local tribes. (1)

Andaman Islands

Chola dynasty referred to this area as Timaittivu; medieval travellers (Marco Polo, Ibn Battuta, Friar Odoric, John de Mandeville) described inhabitants as 'dog-headed cannibals'. (2)

Port Blair was a British penal colony (originally on Chatham Island, later Port Cornwallis); during WWII it briefly served as capital of Subhas Chandra Bose's Azad Hind government. (2)

Cleugh Passage separates Landfall Island from the rest; the Coco Channel separates the Andamans from Myanmar. (1)

Anthropology — Alfred Radcliffe-Brown's 1906 Andaman fieldwork founded structural functionalism. (1)

Indigenous Peoples

Sentinelese — uncontacted Scheduled Tribe on North Sentinel Island; killed American missionary John Allen Chau in 2018; Maurice Vidal Portman once abducted six to Port Blair; first peaceful contact led by Triloknath Pandit. (3)

Onge and Jarawa — other Andamanese tribes, possibly related to the Sentinelese. (2)

Andamanese languages — Boa Sr., the last person who understood the dialect Bo, died in 2010. (1)

Nicobar Islands

Danish missionaries called them Frederiksøerne; Chollan settlers called them the 'Land of the Naked'; settlement was prevented by malaria on Komorta and Nancowry. (1)

Great Nicobar is home to the Shompen people, who speak a language sometimes grouped with Austroasiatic. (1)

Andaman Sea

Names the sea east of the islands; flooded by the Boxing Day Tsunami at resorts including Khao Lak and Phuket. (3)

The Irrawaddy River of Myanmar discharges into this sea; Thailand's Phuket lies along it. (5)

At a Glance

Capital & largest city: Luanda — most populous Lusophone city in the Eastern Hemisphere. (6)

Language: Portuguese — Angola is a major Lusophone state in Africa. (2)

Physical Geography

Kwanza River — namesake of the currency; flows past Luanda's hinterland; receives the Cutato from the Bié Plateau. (3)

Cunene River — flows along the southern border with Namibia; pump station once watered the now-abandoned Tigres Island. (3)

Cubango / Okavango — the Cubango in Angola becomes the Okavango in Botswana, ending in the Okavango Delta. (2)

Tigres Island — became an island in the 1960s when the Atlantic broke through an isthmus. (2)

Cabinda

Cabinda Province — Angola's oil-rich exclave, between the Republic of Congo and the DR Congo; once known as Portuguese Congo; formed from the merger of the N'Goyo, Loango, and Kakongo kingdoms via the Treaty of Simulambuco; capital also called Cabinda, on the Bele River. (4)

Luanda

Agostinho Neto International Airport (Quatro de Fevereiro) — base of national airline TAAG. (2)

Chinese-built suburb of Quilamba Quiaxi (or 'Quilamba'). (2)

Argentina 78
República Argentina · Southern South America · Capital: Buenos Aires
South America

At a Glance

Capital: Buenos Aires — on the western shore of the Río de la Plata, across from Montevideo. (12)

Flag: light-blue / white / light-blue triband with the Sun of May at its center; designed by Manuel Belgrano and made official by the Congress of Tucumán. (3)

Name origin: from Latin argentum ('silver') — the same root names this metal that was mined at Cerro Rico in Potosí. (1)

Extremes: contains both the highest point in the Western/Southern hemispheres (Mount Aconcagua) and the lowest (Laguna del Carbón). (2)

Borders: Chile (along the Andes), Bolivia, Paraguay, Brazil, and Uruguay; second-largest country in South America after Brazil. (6)

Physical Geography

Andes Mountains — form the western border with Chile; run the length of the country and host Mount Aconcagua, Llullaillaco, and the Cordillera Occidental; the world's longest mountain range, running Colombia → Argentina. (4)

Mount Aconcagua — highest mountain in the Southern and Western Hemispheres; near the Chile border; ascended via the Polish Glacier; west face features a 6,000-ft cliff above the Horcones Valley; the Canaleta couloir links the Cresta del Guanaco to its twin summits; name comes from Quechua 'stone sentinel'; cities Uspallata and Mendoza lie on its east flank; closest higher peak is Tirich Mir, over 10,000 mi away. (5)

Patagonia — vast, windy southern region shared with Chile; named by Antonio Pigafetta on Magellan's expedition (claimed to have seen 'giants'); home to the Mapuche and Selk'nam peoples; site of the Conquest of the Desert; contains the Perito Moreno Glacier (whose ruptures dam the Brazo Rico), Mt. Fitz Roy, the Valdés Peninsula (continent's lowest point), and Los Glaciares National Park; bounded north by the Colorado/Barrancas Rivers; subject of Bruce Chatwin's first book In Patagonia; setting of Saint-Exupéry's Night Flight; gave its name to the clothing company. (8)

Pampas — central grassland plains between the Río Colorado and the Salado Norte; home of the gauchos; eastern wet portion (cattle, sheep, estancias) runs Tandil → Mar del Plata; western dry portion (alfalfa, wheat) runs Bahía Blanca → Santa Fé; province of La Pampa has capital Santa Rosa; setting of the epic Martín Fierro. (3)

Gran Chaco — semi-arid lowlands in the north, shared with Bolivia and Paraguay; south of the Pantanal; Pilcomayo River runs through it; backdrop of the Chaco War (Royal Dutch Shell vs. Standard Oil proxy). (3)

Tierra del Fuego — archipelago at South America's southern tip, shared with Chile; largest island separated from Picton/Lennox/Nueva by the Beagle Channel; bounded south by the Drake Passage, north by the Strait of Magellan; Argentine province capital Ushuaia; contains Mount Krund (Cerro Castor ski resort), Fagnano Lake, and the Train of the End of the World; named for Tehuelche bonfires. (5)

Quebrada de Humahuaca — UNESCO gorge of the Río Grande north of San Salvador de Jujuy; ancient Incan trade route; village of Purmamarca known for badlands; extends from the Puña to La Quiaca (across from Villazón, Bolivia). (1)

Iguazú Falls — massive cataract system on a tributary of the Paraná on the Argentina–Brazil border; includes the Garganta del Diablo canyon; drew Eleanor Roosevelt's quip 'Poor Niagara'. (2)

Sand dunes — the world's tallest (named for Federico Kirbus) lies in Argentina's Catamarca Province. (2)

Rivers & Waterways

Río de la Plata — broad estuary forming the border with Uruguay; receives the Paraná and Uruguay Rivers; the Samborombón Bay is its inland-most portion; the Canal Punto Indio is its most navigable; Buenos Aires sits on its western shore. (6)

Paraná River — forms much of the eastern border with Paraguay; flows through the Paraná Delta; sourced in Mato Grosso, Brazil. (3)

Uruguay River — eastern border tributary of the Río de la Plata. (2)

Chubut River — Patagonian river dammed by the Florentino Ameghino Dam. (1)

Lake O'Higgins / San Martín — deepest lake in South America; shared with Chile; named after one of two major generals depending on which side you're on. (1)

Cities & Provinces

Buenos Aires — capital; residents are porteños; lies on the Río de la Plata across from Montevideo. Landmarks: Casa Rosada (Pink House, presidential mansion) on Plaza de Mayo; Teatro Colón opera house; the Kavanagh Building (tallest in Latin America in 1936) in the Retiro barrio; the extremely wide 9 de Julio Avenue with its 235-ft obelisk at Corrientes Avenue; Santiago Calatrava's Puente de la Mujer in Puerto Madero; the colorful Italian La Boca district at the Riachuelo (home of Boca Juniors); the Jewish quarter of Once; the Torre de la Inglaterra; the two-and-a-half-meter-wide Minimal House; Boca Juniors vs. River Plate rivalry; Mothers of the Plaza de Mayo protest group; Lunfardo Italian-based creole; Pope Francis was born here. (9)

Córdoba — second-largest city; named on the flag-clue question alongside Buenos Aires. (1)

Mendoza — wine-region city on the east flank of Aconcagua. (1)

Ushuaia — southernmost city; resort town on the Beagle Channel in Tierra del Fuego province. (2)

Formosa Province — northern province sharing a name with Taiwan. (1)

San Juan Province — contains Ischigualasto Provincial Park (UNESCO site with a complete Triassic fossil record and eoraptor/cynodont fossils), administered with adjacent Talampaya National Park. (1)

Baritu National Park — northern park home to particularly large cedrela trees. (1)

Los Glaciares National Park — contains the country's westernmost point. (1)

Ezeiza Airport — serves Buenos Aires. (1)

Disputed & Outlying Territories

Falkland Islands / Islas Malvinas — South Atlantic archipelago disputed with the UK; capital Stanley; site of the 1982 war after a referendum was rejected by Cristina Fernández de Kirchner's predecessors; East Falkland contains the Lafonia peninsula and Bodie Creek Suspension Bridge; Goose Green and Darwin Harbor named for the Beagle; tallest peak Mount Usborne; third-largest island Weddell Island (world's largest private island); RAF Mount Pleasant connects to Punta Arenas; Christ Church Cathedral in Stanley has a whalebone arch. (6)

Antarctic claims — Argentina claims part of the Antarctic Peninsula (overlapping Chilean and British claims) and the Weddell Sea beyond. (3)

People & Culture

Gauchos — South American cowboys of the Pampas; immortalized in the epic Martín Fierro; estancia ranching culture. (2)

Mapuche — indigenous people of Patagonia; subject of the Araucanización migration south. (2)

Tango — born in Buenos Aires; the march 'La Cumparsita' was popularized as tango in the early 20th century. (1)

Football — Argentina won the 2022 World Cup Final at Lusail; Diego Maradona's 'Hand of God' goal beat England at the Estadio Azteca in the 1986 quarterfinal; the 1986 and 1990 finals; the Boca Juniors / River Plate rivalry in Buenos Aires. (3)

Pope Francis — born Jorge Mario Bergoglio in Buenos Aires. (1)

Pampero winds — cause summer storms in Buenos Aires. (1)

Cueva de las Manos ('Cave of the Hands') — UNESCO site on the Río Pinturas in Patagonia; thousands of stenciled handprints and guanacos painted in kaolin, hematite and natrojarosite by the Toldense people; re-discovered in 1941 by Alberto Maria de Agostini. (2)

Armenia 23
Republic of Armenia · South Caucasus / Transcaucasia · Capital: Yerevan
Caucasus

At a Glance

Capital: Yerevan — on the Hrazdan River, northeast of the Ararat Plain; one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited cities. (4)

Government: former Soviet Republic; in 2018 a Velvet Revolution ousted PM Serzh Sargsyan, replacing him with Nikol Pashinyan. (1)

Religion & Alphabet: Christianity first spread from the Khor Virap Monastery near Lusarat; the Armenian alphabet was developed by Mesrop Mashtots. (1)

Borders: Turkey, Iran (across the Aras River), Georgia, and rival neighbor Azerbaijan; smallest country of the Transcaucasia region. (4)

National Symbols & Disputes

Mount Ararat — Armenia's most powerful national symbol despite lying just across the border in Turkey (Ağrı / Iğdır Provinces); believed landing site of Noah's Ark; first ascended by Friedrich Parrot and Khachatur Abovian; appears on the Armenian Coat of Arms; divided into greater and lesser cones. (4)

Nagorno-Karabakh — autonomous region long disputed with Azerbaijan; the unrecognized Republic of Artsakh held it; the region shrank after Armenia's defeat in the 2020 war; mediation attempted via the Prague Process and Madrid Principles; the Lachin Corridor was given special status under the Bishkek Protocol; Stepanakert is its main city. (4)

Nakhchivan — Azerbaijani exclave sandwiched between Armenia and Iran; one of the two countries occupies the other's enclave village of Karki. (2)

Armenian Genocide — 20th-century Turkish atrocity; refugees gave Yerevan districts their names (Nork Marash, Malatya, Arabkir); the Armenian Quarter of Jerusalem is in part populated by descendants of survivors. (2)

Physical Geography

Caucasus Mountains — Armenia lies in the South Caucasus; the Armenian Highlands lie south of the main range; highest point Mt. Elbrus; legendary site of the Gates of Alexander. (2)

Geghama Mountains — volcanic range east of Yerevan offering hiking trails. (2)

Zangezur Mountains — form the border between Nakhchivan and Armenia's Syunik province. (1)

Lake Sevan — central lake in Gegharkunik Province; one-sixth of the country's area; the only remaining of historic Armenian 'seas' inside the borders; drained by the Hrazdan River and stabilized by a tunnel from the Arpa River; contains the Astvatsatsin Monastery founded by St. Mesrop Mashtots; largest lake in the Caucasus. (3)

Hrazdan River — drains Lake Sevan and flows through Yerevan; site of hydroelectric plants. (4)

Aras River — forms the borders with Iran and Turkey. (2)

Cities, Monasteries & Landmarks

Yerevan — capital; landmarks include Republic Square, the Cascade Complex, and archaeological sites like the Shengavit obelisk and a cuneiform inscription from King Argishti I of Urartu who founded a fortress here; the Dashnak party established a short-lived Democratic Republic here. (4)

Lori Province — home to the medieval Sanahin Monastery. (1)

Kotayk Province — contains the cliffside Geghard Monastery. (1)

Metsamor Nuclear Power Plant — produces roughly 40% of Armenia's electricity. (1)

Khor Virap Monastery — near Lusarat; traditional site where Christianity first took hold. (1)

Armenian Diaspora & Influence

Armenian Quarter of Jerusalem — Old City quarter accessed from the Jaffa Gate; Holy Translators' School was the city's first coeducational school; cathedral where the head of the apostle James is said to be buried (where Kanye West baptized his daughter in 2015); developed in part by Crusader queens Arda, Morphia, and Melisende. (1)

Glendale, California — between Burbank and Pasadena; houses St. Gregory the Illuminator Cathedral, the North American seat of the Armenian Catholic Church. (1)

Aghtamar Island in Lake Van (Turkey) — home to the Armenian Cathedral of the Holy Cross, built by Gagik I Artsruni. (1)

Julfa (in Nakhchivan) — once held the world's largest collection of khachkar gravestones before their destruction in the 1990s. (1)

Region: The 'brother' state of India's Seven Sister States of the NE. (1)

Highest point of India: Kangchenjunga — the world's 3rd-tallest mountain; partly in Sikkim. (3)

Language: A majority of residents speak Nepali. (1)

Distinction: Chief producer of cardamom in Asia. (1)

Geography

Kangchenjunga — delineated by the Tamur and Teesta Rivers; tourists view its SE face from the Goecha La pass; held the title 'highest in the world' until 1852. (2)

Nathu La — Sikkim's 'listening ears pass'; once traversed by the Ancient Tea Horse Road. (1)

The Brahmaputra basin (above Sikkim, in Tibet) feeds Kanchenjunga's drainage on the Indian side. (1)

Government & Environment

The state's traditional monarch was known as the Chogyal, who lived in Gangtok. (1)

Sikkim has a unilateral ban on styrofoam and has distributed bamboo water bottles to prevent plastic pollution. (2)

Brazil (States & Regions)

São Paulo (city)

Atlantic link: connected to the coast (Port of Santos, 30 mi south) via the Anchieta and Imigrantes highway systems. (1)

Guarulhos International Airport — second-busiest in Latin America. (1)

Tamil Nadu 19
Southernmost mainland Indian state · Capital: Chennai
India (States & Regions)

At a Glance

Capital: Chennai (historic Madras) — on the Coromandel Coast of the Bay of Bengal. (4)

Southernmost mainland point of India: Kanyakumari (Cape Comorin), in this state. (1)

Borders: Kerala (W), Karnataka (NW), Andhra Pradesh (N); the union territory of Puducherry is embedded within it. (3)

Strait: Palk Strait separates the state from Sri Lanka; Adam's Bridge (a chain of limestone shoals) once connected the two until a 1480 cyclone. (3)

Major cities: Chennai, Coimbatore, Madurai — Madurai nicknamed 'Thoonga Nagaram' ('the city that never sleeps'). (2)

Physical Geography

Rivers — the Kaveri (Cauvery) splits into distributaries such as the Kollidam; the Vaigai empties into the Palk Strait. (2)

Mountains — contains the junction of the Eastern and Western Ghats. (1)

Cauvery dispute — Tamil Nadu and Karnataka have feuded for decades over the river's waters. (1)

Telugu Ganga — a project from neighbouring Andhra Pradesh that diverts Krishna River water to Chennai. (1)

Chennai (Madras)

Fort St. George — historic British East India Company fort, foundational structure of Madras. (1)

George Town — historic neighbourhood; contains Parry's Corner port district, named for an 18th-c. Welsh merchant. (1)

Ripon Building (neoclassical, foundation laid by Lord Minto), Dr. M.G.R. Railway Station, ITC Grand Chola Hotel, Adyar Cancer Institute. (1)

Mahabalipuram — UNESCO temple complex just south of the city. (1)

Tamil cinema (Kollywood) — based in Chennai; produced political stars including Vijayakanth and Jayaram Jayalalithaa. (2)

People & Culture

Tamil people — Dravidian-speaking ethnic group of the south; built the absurdly colorful Meenakshi Temple at Madurai; literary Sangam period predates the Five Great Epics (incl. Ilango Adigal's 'Tale of an Anklet'). (2)

Historic empires — Chera, Chola, Pallava — Tamil-speaking states. (2)

'Eelam' — proposed Tamil homeland; LTTE insurgency in Sri Lanka was conducted by ethnic Tamils. (2)

Sri Ranganathaswamy Temple — on an island between the Kollidam and Kaveri rivers; the world's largest functioning Hindu temple. (1)

Puducherry (Pondicherry)

French colonial city embedded as a UT inside Tamil Nadu; landmarks include the Romain Rolland Library and the Notre Dame des Anges cathedral; royalist bastion under governor Venant de Durfort. (1)

Tasmania 7
Island State of Australia · Capital: Hobart · Formerly Van Diemen's Land
Australia (States & Regions)

At a Glance

Capital: Hobart — on the Derwent River, beneath Mount Wellington. (7)

Originally named Van Diemen's Land (for a Dutch East India Company leader); renamed for Dutch explorer Abel Tasman. (3)

Smallest Australian state by area; lies south of the Bass Strait at the southern end of the Indian/Pacific Ocean boundary above the Southern Ocean. (4)

Cities: Launceston in the north; Penguin and Devonport on the north coast; Strahan is a west-coast fishing town; Clarence is on the Coal River. (2)

Physical Geography

Mount Ossa — Tasmania's highest point, in the Pellion Range. (2)

King David's Peak — in Walls of Jerusalem National Park. (1)

Derwent River — descends from Mt. Ossa to Storm Bay, passing Hobart and Montague Bay. (3)

Esk River — drains the north. (1)

Lake St. Clair — the country's deepest lake. (1)

Maria Island — off the east coast; Flinders Island lies in Bass Strait (Truganini and other natives were evacuated there by George Augustus Robinson); Savage River National Park in the west. (2)

Forestier Peninsula — site of the colonial-era 'Black Line'. (1)

Upper Florentine Valley — old-growth forest at the center of logging controversy. (1)

Wildlife

Tasmanian devil (Sarcophilus harrisii) — largest extant carnivorous marsupial; native to the island. (2)

Thylacine ('Tasmanian tiger') — Australia's largest carnivorous marsupial until extinction in 1936. (2)

Culture & History

Museum of Old and New Art (MONA) — the Southern Hemisphere's largest private collection; on the Derwent; houses the controversial Cloaca machine; accessed by ferry. (2)

Port Arthur — historic convict settlement; site of a 1996 mass shooting that led to Australia's strict gun-control laws. (2)

Incat — catamaran manufacturer based here. (1)

Par-Avion airline is based in the state. (1)

Murchison Highway crosses Hellyer Gorge; John Franklin briefly governed the colony. (1)

Uttar Pradesh 14
Largest state in India · Capital: Lucknow
India (States & Regions)

At a Glance

Capital: Lucknow. (1)

Status: The world's most populous national subdivision; the largest state of India. (1)

In 2000, Uttarakhand broke away from Uttar Pradesh as a separate state. (1)

Major cities: Lucknow, Agra, Varanasi, Allahabad (Prayagraj), Kanpur, Ayodhya, Chitrakoot. (2)

Setting: Lies in the Ganges Plain; the Ganges and Yamuna both flow through it. (4)

Sacred Geography

Varanasi — sacred Hindu city on the Ganges where pilgrims cremate their dead on the river's banks; one of the Saptapuri (with Ayodhya, Kanchipuram). (4)

Ayodhya — on the Sarayu River; considered the birthplace of Rama. (2)

Allahabad (Prayag/Prayagraj) — built on the plan of ancient Prayag; one of the four sites of the Kumbh Mela pilgrimage. (1)

Agra

Taj Mahal — Mughal-era mausoleum built by Shah Jahan for his wife; white-marble onion domes; the only New Seven Wonder in South Asia; sits on a platform along the Yamuna River with the Mehtab Bagh gardens opposite. (3)

Bibi Ka Maqbara — half-size replica built by Aurangzeb a decade after the Taj. (1)

Modern issues — green stains from Chironomus calligraphus insects swarming on the Taj, traced to pollution in the Yamuna. (1)

Lucknow

Rumi Darwaza — 'the Turkish Gate', once mistakenly believed identical to a gate in Constantinople. (1)

Ghanta Ghar — the tallest clock tower in India. (1)

Dilkusha Kothi — ruins of a British residence besieged during the Indian Rebellion of 1857. (1)

History & Other

Predecessor entity: United Provinces of Agra and Audh. (1)

Rihand River — power generated in part by a coal plant named for it. (1)

Jalesar — town in UP that produces cast brass bells popular with Swiss cow farmers. (1)

JRDU (Chitrakoot) — world's first university dedicated solely to teaching disabled students. (1)

Rod Laver Arena — principal venue of the Australian Open tennis Grand Slam, built on Flinders Park. (1)

Melbourne Cricket Ground — in Yarra Park; largest stadium in the Southern Hemisphere; home of the Melbourne Cricket Club. (2)

State Geography

Ballarat — site of a 19th-century gold rush. (1)

Western Australia 6
State of Australia · Capital: Perth · Largest state
Australia (States & Regions)

At a Glance

Capital: Perth — port city of 2.3 million; world's most isolated state capital; on the Swan River / Swan Estuary; named for a city on the Firth of Tay in Scotland. (4)

Flag: features a black swan — referencing Dutch explorers' 1697 discovery of black swans on the Swan River. (1)

Stamps: government accidentally issued the blue 'inverted swan' (1855), one of the world's earliest invert errors. (1)

Rabbit-proof fence: crossed entirely by the 2,000-mile fence since 1907. (1)

Perth & Surroundings

Rottnest Island — habitat of the smiley macropod quokka (also found on Albany's Bald Island). (3)

Fremantle — Perth's port, at the mouth of the Swan River. (3)

Suburbs & beaches: Mandurah, Kwinana, Welshpool; Cottesloe Beach; bounded east by the Darling Escarpment. (2)

National Parks: John Forrest and Walyunga in the Darling Range. (1)

Christmas Island — Indian Ocean territory accessed via Perth. (1)

State Geography & Mining

Pilbara — remote iron-ore region in the northwest. (1)

Great Sandy Desert — arid expanse in the state's north. (1)

Shark Bay — UNESCO-listed bay known for stromatolites and dugongs. (1)

Mines: Argyle Diamond Mine (world's largest), Woodie Woodie manganese mine; gold rushes at Halls Creek (1885) and Kalgoorlie (1893); the Goldfields Water Supply Scheme pipeline ran to Coolgardie. (2)
